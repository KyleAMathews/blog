# **Primitive Accumulation: Field Notes on Pre-Contact World-Machines**

*A story set in Iain M. Banks's Culture universe. The Culture is a post-scarcity, galaxy-spanning civilization of humans and artificial intelligences, millennia more advanced than present-day Earth. Their policy toward less-developed civilizations is typically observation without intervention. This story follows a Culture agent assigned to monitor Earth across seven centuries of human history—from the medieval trade networks of 1287 CE through the present day—as she watches humanity build the machines of modernity.*

## **DISPATCH I: Quanzhou, 1287 CE (Local)**

The harbour at Quanzhou smelled of fish, tar, human waste, and that particular desperation that accumulates wherever large sums of money change hands quickly. I rather liked it. The *Fastidious*—I use the short form; life is too brief for the full name, and I've been told I lack gravitas anyway—had suggested I might find the sensory environment "overwhelming." The *Fastidious* had never, to my knowledge, inhabited a body capable of being overwhelmed by anything, and I suspected it was projecting.

The drone *Just Checking* floated beside me, fully invisible. It had been in a mood since we'd left the ship—something about the assignment being beneath its capabilities. This was not an entirely unreasonable complaint, given that *Just Checking* was rated for fleet combat and had participated in five major engagements that still featured in tactical training simulations. It had requested this assignment anyway, for reasons it declined to discuss, which I found more interesting than the complaint.

"Explain to me again," it said, "why we're here instead of literally anywhere else in the galaxy."

"Professional interest."

"You have professional interest in a harbour that smells like rotting mackerel."

"I have professional interest in what's happening *in* the harbour. Also, it's not rotting. It's fermenting. They make a sauce from it. Quite good, actually."

"You've *eaten* it?"

"Several times. There's a vendor on the Street of Ten Thousand Fragrances who does remarkable things with noodles."

The drone made a sound I had learned to interpret as profound spiritual exhaustion. "I was built for fleet engagements. I have *engaged* in fleet engagements. I have conducted operations that shaped the destiny of—" It stopped itself. "Never mind. What am I supposed to be looking at?"

"Down there. The wharf reserved for foreign vessels."

Below us, a ship-captain from Basra was negotiating with a Tamil factor over credit instruments drawn on a Gujarati banking house. Neither spoke the other's language with any fluency. They were using a pidgin that incorporated Arabic, Tamil, and Hokkien, supplemented by hand gestures. The negotiation concerned goods that were currently six weeks away by sea.

"I see two men haggling," *Just Checking* said. "I've seen haggling. It's not interesting."

"Look harder."

The drone was quiet for a moment. Its sensor fields expanded—not visibly, but I could feel the faint tingle of its attention intensifying, sweeping across the harbour, the city, the trade routes that converged here from half a world away.

When it spoke again, its voice had shifted—still sardonic, but with an undertone I hadn't heard before.

"They're using credit instruments that function across religious and linguistic barriers. The Tamil's paper is backed by a Hindu banking house but will be honoured by Muslim merchants in Hormuz. The Basran is calculating exchange rate arbitrage across three currency systems without any formal training in what we'd call economics." A pause. "That's... moderately clever."

"It's more than moderately clever. They've built a planetary coordination system. Eight overlapping trade circuits, three major subsystems, no hegemon. Distributed control, emergent integration, self-correcting feedback loops." I paused, watching a ship from somewhere in Southeast Asia manoeuvre toward an empty berth. "A world-machine."

"A world-machine they don't know they've built."

"That's what makes it interesting."

We made our way from the docks to a teahouse I had used before, one with a balcony overlooking the harbour.

I should explain how I came to be here, watching humans while a war-drone complained about the smell.

I had been Contact for thirty-seven years, give or take. One prior assignment: a routine observation of a developing civilization in the Greater Magellanic Cloud—pleasant enough people, terrible cuisine, a political system based on hereditary interpretive dance that I still didn't fully understand. The assignment had ended badly. I preferred not to think about it.

Earth was my second posting, and it was supposed to be a rest assignment. The Sol system was flagged as unusual—the Referee decision of 873 CE noted "patterns of spontaneous systemic emergence that warrant extended observation"—but unusual in the sense of academically interesting, not operationally demanding. Some Mind, somewhere, had gotten curious about how these particular humans were going to work out. I was sent to satisfy that curiosity.

The *Fastidious* had prepared me well. Six months of modifications before I ever set foot on the planet: two extra toes to match local anatomy, a joint removed from each finger, cheekbones and ears resculpted. My drug glands had been recalibrated for a biochemistry that couldn't access our usual pharmacopeia—no glanding calm in a crisis, no switching off pain, no chemical edge when I needed one. The ship had also taught me to walk differently; Culture citizens moved wrong, something in the gait that registered as uncanny to baseline humans. I looked down at my hands, wrapped around the teacup. Still mine, but subtly off—the fingers too short, the knuckles in unfamiliar places.

I had not expected to become curious myself. That was the problem with Contact work: you spent enough time watching a species, and eventually they became *people* to you, with all the complications that entailed.

"Brief me," I said—subvocalizing, the words traveling through my neural lace to the drone. The lace handled languages: I had absorbed Hokkien, Arabic, and Tamil in the weeks before arrival, the vocabularies and grammars flowering in my mind while I slept. To anyone watching, I was simply a foreign woman drinking tea and watching the harbour. "I want to hear how you see it. Full configuration."

"You've been here longer than I have. You don't need me to—"

"I want your analysis. You see patterns I miss. Impress me."

"I live to serve," the drone said, in a tone suggesting it lived for nothing of the kind. "Eight circuits, three subsystems. European subsystem: least developed. The Champagne fairs—seasonal markets in the French interior—serve as the primary exchange hub. Venice and Genoa are fighting over control of the Mediterranean chokepoint, by which I mean they've had four major wars so far and are gearing up for more. On the positive side, both have developed republican governance, sophisticated accounting practices, and what we might generously call intelligence services. Less positively, they keep trying to burn each other's fleets."

"Middle East?"

"Two routes. Northern overland through Mongol Central Asia—currently stable, which by local standards means the mass killings have paused for administrative reorganization. Southern maritime through the Red Sea and Cairo. Cairo's enormous—hundreds of thousands of people, hard to say exactly with their census methods. They count households, not individuals, and their definition of 'household' appears to involve theological considerations I haven't fully parsed. Largest city west of China, probably. It serves as the primary junction between Mediterranean and Indian Ocean trade."

*Just Checking* projected a schematic visible only to me—trade routes rendered as luminous threads connecting nodes of varying intensity. The pattern was beautiful, in the way that functional systems often were: organic, emergent, shaped by forces no designer had specified.

"And Asia?"

"Most developed. India functions as the pivot—connected to everyone, equidistant from everything. The Strait of Malacca controls all traffic between Indian and Pacific oceans; whoever dominates it essentially taxes half the planet's trade. Right now that's a mess of competing claims—post-Srivijaya fragmentation, local powers, pirates who are also sometimes traders who are also sometimes officials. Nobody's consolidated it yet. And China—" The drone's voice shifted again, taking on something that might have been reluctant respect. "China's the interesting part."

"Go on."

"This port, Quanzhou, is among the busiest on the planet—arguably *the* greatest emporium on the maritime routes. The Song dynasty—well, it's Yuan now, Mongol conquest, long story—developed state-organized convoys, standardized weights and measures, regulatory tariffs, a functioning court system for commercial disputes. Their navy, at peak, operated ships that dwarf anything the Europeans are currently sailing—two, three times the displacement. And they invented paper currency."

"Paper currency? At this tech level?"

"Backed by metal reserves in theory. Redeemable under conditions that keep being suspended, in practice. The current regime has been debasing it for decades. The trajectory is unsustainable—at this rate, the system will face a serious crisis within a few generations." The drone made a sound that might have been amusement, or might have been something darker. "They invented fiat money hundreds of years before they had the institutional capacity to manage it. Very educational."

I looked out over the harbour. The Arab quarter was visible from here—mosques and warehouses, a whole community of foreign merchants who had made this port their home. The city had welcomed them, more or less. Given them space to practice their religion, to govern their own affairs within limits, to become part of the machine without being absorbed by it.

"Brief me on the document flows," I said.

"The what?"

"The paper. The contracts, the letters of credit, the correspondence. Where does it accumulate?"

*Just Checking* was quiet for a moment, processing. "Cairo has the densest archives. There's a community there—Jewish merchants—who've been saving commercial correspondence for generations. Bills of lading, IOUs, letters between trading partners across the whole Indian Ocean system. They store it in a chamber attached to their synagogue. They call it a geniza."

"A what?"

"A repository for documents that contain the name of God—they can't destroy them, so they store them. But they've been putting everything in there. Commercial records, personal letters, shopping lists. It's essentially the memory of the western half of your world-machine, sitting in a room in Cairo."

I filed that away. A system that built its own archive without meaning to. A distributed intelligence that was also, accidentally, recording itself.

"Show me the map again," I said. "The full configuration."

The drone projected it: eight circuits, three subsystems, the threads of trade and credit and information that bound this fragmented world into something that functioned as a whole. No one had designed it. No one controlled it. No one fully understood it.

And yet it worked.

"They're going to build something on top of this," I said, half to myself. "Something bigger. More legible."

"Legible?"

"Readable. Countable. Standardized." I watched the Tamil factor and the Basran captain conclude their negotiations, each offering the other a slight bow. "Right now the machine runs on reputation and relationship. Local knowledge. Personal trust. That works at this scale, but it won't scale further. If they're going to coordinate at planetary level, they'll need something more systematic. Ways to make the world enumerable."

"You're predicting a phase transition."

"I'm noting that the current configuration has limits. What comes next—" I shrugged. "That's what we're here to watch."


---

I spent the next several weeks moving through the system's major nodes, with *Just Checking* maintaining its usual invisible escort and providing a running commentary that alternated between genuine insight and theatrical complaint.

Cairo, where the Mamluk sultanate had created an administrative framework that would have impressed a Culture efficiency analyst. The Mamluks were slave-soldiers who had seized power from their masters, replacing clean hereditary succession with something messier—factional military selection, power flowing to whoever could assemble enough support among the competing blocs. The system had obvious failure modes: succession crises, internal conflict, a tendency to solve political problems through assassination. But it had also produced a stable, prosperous state that controlled the junction between Mediterranean and Indian Ocean trade.

I found the geniza *Just Checking* had mentioned—or rather, I found the synagogue that housed it, and contrived a reason to be admitted. The chamber was remarkable: centuries of accumulated paper, layered like geological strata, a cross-section of commercial life across half a world. Letters in Arabic and Hebrew and Judeo-Arabic, contracts witnessed in multiple cities, the fossilized traces of transactions that had built the system I was studying.

"This is the machine's hippocampus," I said, half to myself.

"What?"

"Memory storage. The system is recording itself. Not intentionally—no one decided to build an archive of Indian Ocean trade. But the practices that make the system work—written contracts, correspondence networks, institutional continuity—they generate records as a byproduct. The machine remembers what it's done even if no one is trying to remember."

Venice, where a Doge had just died and the succession was underway.

We had studied the system, of course. Thirty electors chosen by lot, reduced to nine, who select forty, reduced to twelve, who select twenty-five, reduced to nine, who select forty-five, reduced to eleven, who select the final forty-one electors. Seven rounds of sortition and selection, specifically designed to shatter any pre-arranged faction before it could capture the outcome.

On paper, it was elegant. In the Sala del Maggior Consiglio—a hall vast enough to hold fifteen hundred patricians, Guariento's Paradise fresco looming above the empty throne—watching it happen, it was chaos.

Below me, thirty men sat in uncomfortable silence while a boy of perhaps ten approached the urn on its pedestal. The *ballottino*—grabbed that morning from the Piazza San Marco, a commoner's child selected precisely for his ignorance of the men whose fates he would determine. Each patrician drew a linen ball from the urn. Nine drew gold; twenty-one drew silver. The silver-drawers filed out.

Then the nine remaining huddled to argue about which forty names should advance. Two factions had clearly formed—I could see it in how they clustered, in who refused to meet whose eyes. The argument grew heated. A man in crimson robes stood and walked to the window, staring out at the lagoon while the others shouted.

"This is not matching my model," *Just Checking* said. "The system is supposed to prevent faction formation. There are *obviously* factions."

"Keep watching."

The forty new electors were finally chosen. They filed in. The boy returned with his urn. Twelve drew gold. The twelve argued—but the alliances had shifted. The crimson-robed man was now allied with someone he'd been ignoring an hour before. They selected twenty-five. The twenty-five reduced to nine. The nine selected forty-five.

"The factions keep breaking," the drone said slowly. "Every time they solidify, the next round scrambles them."

By the fourth day, men who had entered as allies were barely speaking. The original factions had been broken apart, recombined, broken again. When the final vote came, it went twenty-seven to fourteen—a comfortable margin for a man neither original faction had championed.

"Ah," *Just Checking* said.

"You see it now."

"The system doesn't prevent faction formation. It *assumes* factions and then grinds them apart before they can matter." The drone's field dimmed to something like respect. "An immune system. They've evolved an immune system against capture."

"Centuries of trial and error. No formal theory. Just accumulated scar tissue from every attack." I watched the new Doge receive the *corno*—the ducal cap—while the man in crimson robes applauded with everyone else, his earlier anger invisible. "Every other republic they know has fallen to strongmen. This one keeps surviving."

"And they have no idea what they've built."

"They know it works. That's enough."

Gujarat, where Hindu and Muslim merchants operated side by side under legal frameworks that both found mutually acceptable if not ideal. I spent a week in Cambay, watching the rhythms of a port that served as the western anchor of the Indian Ocean system.

The merchant I spoke with most often was a Jain named Vastupal, whose family had been trading across the ocean for six generations. He was old, wealthy, and philosophical—the combination that tends to produce the most interesting conversations in any civilization.

"You are not from here," he said, during our third meeting, over cups of a sweet milk-based drink I had developed a genuine fondness for. "You are not from anywhere I know. Your accent is strange. Your questions are stranger."

I had prepared cover stories for this contingency. But something in his eyes made me hesitate.

"Does it matter?" I asked.

"That depends." He didn't look away. "On what you want."

"To understand. How this—" I gestured at the harbor, the ships, the flow of goods and people. "How it works."

"Many people want to understand how trade works. Tax collectors. Pirates. Rival houses." He took a sip of his drink. "You do not ask like any of them. You ask like someone who has seen other markets, other ways, and is comparing."

I said nothing. His eyes were sharp, patient—a man who had spent sixty years reading people across language barriers.

"I will tell you a story," he said finally. "My grandfather traded with a Muslim house in Calicut for forty years. Last month I advanced silver to a Persian in Aden—a man I have never met—for a shipment of indigo I will not see for eight months. I do not share his god or his language. But I know he will pay, because if he does not, no one in any port from here to Canton will trade with him again."

"Reputation as collateral."

"Better than collateral. A warehouse can burn. A ship can sink. Reputation—" He spread his hands. "Reputation lives in a thousand heads at once. You cannot steal it. You can only lose it." He smiled, showing teeth stained red from betel nut. "My grandfather and the Calicut house cheated each other twice—once each, both small matters. They also saved each other from ruin three times. At his funeral, their eldest son wept."

He set down his cup and looked at me directly.

"Now. You have seen other systems. Tell me—what doctrine explains that?"

I held his gaze. He wasn't asking rhetorically. He was testing whether I would give him something real in exchange for what he'd offered.

"I don't know of a doctrine," I said carefully. "But I've seen the pattern before. People who should be enemies, learning to need each other. It seems to... grow. Without anyone planting it."

He nodded slowly. "Grow without planting. Yes. That is a good way to say it." He picked up his cup again. "I do not know where you are from. I do not think you will tell me. But you see clearly, and that is enough."

Later, walking through the evening streets with *Just Checking* manifesting as a faint shimmer at my shoulder, I found myself returning to the conversation.

"He almost sees it," I said. "The system. The world-machine. He's been operating inside it for sixty years, and he's starting to perceive its outlines."

"He described trade networks. He didn't describe emergent planetary coordination."

"He described trade networks creating something larger than themselves. That's the first step." I paused, watching a ship's lanterns move slowly across the dark water of the harbour. "Give them a few more centuries and they'll have the theoretical vocabulary to articulate what he intuited."

But what interested me most was what the machine had made of Vastupal himself. Six generations of cross-cultural dealing had shaped a particular kind of person — patient, cosmopolitan, extending trust across lines that should have been barriers. The harbour towns were full of people like him. The hinterlands were not. The machine's most important output wasn't the trade. It was the *traders*.

"Or they'll destroy themselves first."

"That's always the question, isn't it?"

## **DISPATCH II: Seville, 1519 CE (Local)**

The machine broke.

I returned to Earth after two hundred thirty-two years away—other assignments, a particularly tedious trade negotiation in the Lesser Cloud that I won't bore you with. Most Contact agents rotate off long-term observation posts after a century or two. I had requested reassignment back to Sol. The *Fastidious* had opinions about this but approved it anyway. The *Gravitas Shortfall* provided a briefing during final approach, but I didn't need it. *Just Checking* summarized in the time it took us to drop from orbit to the Iberian coast.

"Bacterial pandemic. *Yersinia pestis*. Originated in Central Asian rodent populations, transmitted to humans via flea vectors. It spread through the trade networks you admired so much in your last report."

"Mortality?"

"Between thirty and sixty percent at every major node, depending on local conditions. Higher in cities. Lower in isolated rural areas." A pause. "The merchant houses you documented? Half of them gone. The trade routes you mapped? Operating at a fraction of 1287 capacity. The geniza in Cairo is still accumulating documents, but the volume has dropped. Fewer transactions to record."

I had known this was coming. The *Fastidious* had provided projections before I left, two centuries ago. The integration I had admired was also a transmission vector; the same ships that carried silk carried rats. The machine worked exactly as designed. It just wasn't designed to handle that particular input.

Knowing didn't help.

"The system collapsed?" I asked, keeping my voice neutral.

"Partially. The Mongol khanates fragmented—succession crises compounded by population loss. The overland routes are mostly closed. China turned inward; the Ming expelled the Mongols but also shut down the maritime expeditions. The Indian Ocean networks survived better, but they're diminished."

"And Europe?"

"That's why we're here."


---

The harbour at Seville was chaos—organized chaos, the kind that signified massive capital flows and inadequate infrastructure. Ships were being outfitted for expeditions to continents that, two hundred years ago, had been unknown to the Eurasian system. The Spanish had stumbled across an entirely separate world, and they were in the process of—

*Just Checking* supplied the word: "Integrating."

"That's one way to put it."

"The polite way. The accurate way involves more references to genocide, slavery, and ecological catastrophe." The drone's voice had an edge I hadn't heard before. "The returning ships bring gold and unfamiliar diseases. The outgoing ships carry weapons, missionaries, and diseases far more lethal to their recipients. Current demographic projections for the indigenous American populations suggest eighty to ninety percent mortality within a century and a half."

"From disease?"

"Primarily. Smallpox, measles, typhus, influenza—they have no immunity. The military conquest is almost secondary. By the time the Spanish armies arrive, the populations they're conquering have already been decimated by pathogens that traveled faster than any army could."

I watched the preparations from a rooftop overlooking the river. A fleet was being assembled for an expedition that aimed to circumnavigate the globe. The commander, a Portuguese navigator named Magellan sailing under the Spanish flag, was recruiting sailors with promises of spices and glory.

"The loop closes," I said. "That's what this is. The 1287 machine had gaps—the Americas, the Pacific. This expedition closes them. After this, every part of the planet is connected to every other part."

"Connected via Spanish galleons, which are primarily useful for moving silver, slaves, and smallpox." *Just Checking*'s voice was flat. "The machine is getting bigger. It's not getting gentler."

Below us, a different kind of traffic: enslaved Africans being led toward ships bound for the Caribbean. The Spanish had discovered that American indigenous populations died too quickly to be useful as forced labour. African populations, with some resistance to European diseases, lasted longer. The logic was brutal and, from a certain analytical perspective, economically rational.

"They're building a new machine," I said. "Different architecture from 1287."

"Very different. The 1287 system integrated existing production networks—it connected what was already there. This one is reorganizing production from scratch to serve external demand. Encomienda labour in the Caribbean. Sugar plantations that didn't exist a generation ago. Gold extraction wherever they can find it." The drone paused. "And they're just getting started. If there are significant mineral deposits in those continents—and geography suggests there will be—this extraction machine will scale by orders of magnitude."

"Extraction rather than exchange."

"With the humans who lived here as the primary resource being extracted. Them, the silver, the land, the ecosystems—everything that isn't nailed down." The drone's voice was flat. "Unusual. Most species reserve this treatment for competitors they don't recognize as persons."


---

A Dominican friar named Bartolomé de las Casas had appointed himself advocate for the indigenous populations. He had spent seventeen years in the Caribbean colonies. He had seen what was happening. He had returned to Spain to argue, before church councils and royal courts, that the Indians were fully human and deserved legal protection.

I arranged a conversation. He received me in a small room adjoining the monastery, crowded with papers and books and the accumulated evidence of a man building a legal case against an empire.

He was perhaps fifty, but looked older. His hands trembled slightly as he moved papers aside to make space for me, and twice during our conversation he lost the thread of what he was saying and stared at the wall as if seeing something else entirely. When a door slammed somewhere in the monastery, he flinched.

"You understand," I said, "that your position faces significant opposition. The economic interests are considerable."

"I watched them feed infants to their dogs." His voice was hoarse, barely above a whisper. "For sport. For *sport*. I watched them work men to death in the mines and call it Christian labor. I watched—" He stopped. His hands were shaking. "Seventeen years. I saw what we did in Christ's name. What we are still doing."

"The Council argues that—"

"The Council." He laughed—a terrible sound, more cough than mirth. "The Council debates whether the Indians have souls. I could have saved them the trouble. I watched a mother drown her children rather than let them be taken. Tell me that creature lacked a soul. Tell me to my face."

He was breathing hard now. I waited.

"They are rational beings," he said, quieter, finding the legal formula like a man gripping a rope in a storm. "Capable of receiving the faith. Possessed of natural rights that no conquest can annul. This is not my opinion—it is Scripture, it is Aquinas, it is the teaching of the Church from the beginning." His eyes met mine. "I know the economic interests. I know the opposition. I know I will probably fail. But I also know that every word I write here will survive. Every atrocity I document will be remembered." He gestured at the papers surrounding us. "There will be a reckoning. There is always a reckoning."


---

Later, I described the exchange to *Just Checking*, walking through the Seville evening.

"He's trying to steer the machine," I said. "He doesn't have the power to stop it. But he's trying to introduce friction, to create accountability mechanisms, to make it harder for the operators to ignore what they're doing."

"Is it working?"

"Not enough. But it's not nothing." I stopped walking, staring at my reflection in the dark water of the Guadalquivir. "He arrived at the correct conclusion."

"Presumably not through rigorous application of Bayesian inference."

"Through Catholic theology, actually. That's what interests me." I started walking again. "He found his way to something we'd affirm without hesitation, through a framework we wouldn't have expected to get him there. It keeps happening here, and I don't fully understand why."

"Or," *Just Checking* said, "you're exhibiting classic selection bias. You notice the Las Casases because they confirm what you already believe. For every one of him, there are probably ten equally sophisticated thinkers concluding that genocide builds character. You just don't find them as interesting."

"So the pattern's just my confirmation bias?"

"Possibly. You're looking for people you'd want to have dinner with. Naturally you find them."

I considered this. "Except they keep turning up independently. Different centuries, different continents, different starting points—same conclusions. That's either a remarkable coincidence or there's something structural going on."

"Then what's your explanation?"

I didn't have one. Not yet. But I was beginning to suspect that the answer had something to do with the machine itself—that certain kinds of coordination problems had limited solution spaces, and sufficiently complex minds kept finding the same answers because there weren't that many answers to find.

We walked in silence for a while. The evening streets were quieter now. A group of sailors passed, drunk, singing something obscene about the Admiral's wife.

"There's something else," I said finally. "Something I noticed in the briefing data. The Portuguese in the Indian Ocean—they've been burning cities. Bombarding ports that traded peacefully for centuries. The Malacca Strait, Calicut, Hormuz. The old Trade Machine's nodes."

"I saw the reports."

"And the Spanish here. What they're doing in the Americas. And what they did in Granada, before that—expelling the Jews, the Muslims, anyone who didn't fit the new configuration." I stopped walking.

"Your point?"

"The old machine is dying. The Trade Machine—the thing I documented in 1287, with its distributed trust and local knowledge—it's being dismantled. But whatever's replacing it isn't working yet. The new configuration is under construction. It doesn't have the infrastructure to coordinate what it's trying to coordinate."

*Just Checking* was quiet, waiting.

"And in the gap between?" I said. "Monsters. The Portuguese running amok because the old rules don't apply and the new rules don't exist yet. The Spanish treating entire continents as resources to be extracted because there's no framework to tell them otherwise. Every restraint that the Trade Machine had developed—the reputation networks, the shared expectations, the relationships that made traders into something like neighbors—gone. And nothing to replace them except raw power and the appetite for gold."

"You're saying the violence isn't incidental."

## **DISPATCH III: The Legibility Machine (1687-1950 CE)**

I returned to Earth multiple times over the next three and a half centuries, cycling between the planet and other postings via the *Fastidious*—ninety kilometres of drifting habitats at the system's edge, component sections strung together by field-locks, each containing its own climate and vegetation. Between visits the ship refreshed my modifications, briefed me on what I'd missed, rebuilt what needed rebuilding. I slept in a forest imported from a world whose name I couldn't pronounce, woke to artificial sunlight, and went back down to watch the machine being completed.

What follows is a compressed account of those visits.

The machine I had glimpsed in Seville was being completed.


### **Amsterdam, 1689 CE**

The smell hit me first—tobacco smoke and ink and wool and the particular musk of men who had been shouting since the bell rang at eleven. Then the noise: three hundred voices in Dutch, German, Portuguese, Spanish, Yiddish—the Sephardim in their corner, the Ashkenazim in theirs, a babel of commerce that somehow resolved into prices. "I don't know yet."

"Emergent computation," I said finally. "They've built a calculator for economic optimization, running on human wetware and paper records."

"They're already theorizing the system," I said. "Not just operating it—analyzing it. De la Vega is watching the machine and trying to understand how it works."

A lens-grinder named Spinoza had died ten years earlier. His books were banned across Europe; his ideas were considered dangerous enough that the Jewish community of Amsterdam had excommunicated him and the Christian authorities had condemned his works to burning. And yet his followers still gathered, still corresponded, still quietly circulated manuscripts of his *Ethics*.

I attended one of their meetings, presenting myself as a foreign scholar with an interest in natural philosophy. The group met in a back room, shutters drawn, voices low. One of them told me a story. In 1672, when the de Witt brothers—Spinoza's friends and patrons—had been lynched by a mob in The Hague, Spinoza had wanted to go out that night and post a sign at the site of the murders. Just two words: *Ultimi barbarorum*. The ultimate barbarians.

"His landlord locked him in the house," the man said. "He would have been torn apart."

I thought about that image: a philosopher who had derived, through pure logic, that all of reality was one substance—and who still felt compelled to confront a mob with a sign. The gap between understanding and action. The question of how people who live by reason can exist among those driven by passion.

"What did he conclude?" I asked. "About how to bridge that gap?"

The man smiled slightly. "He concluded that you don't bridge it through persuasion. You build institutions."

That was what interested me most about Spinoza's political philosophy. What interested me was his answer to a practical problem: how do you coordinate people who will never agree?

His solution: don't try to change their beliefs. Design systems that channel their passions toward collective benefit. Separate inner conviction from outer conduct. Let people think whatever they want, as long as the institutions produce coordination.

"It's the opposite of the Legibility Machine," I said to *Just Checking* afterward, walking through the Amsterdam evening. "The Legibility Machine tries to make everyone the same so they can be coordinated. Spinoza says: let them be different. Build the coordination into the structure, not the people."

"Tolerance as *pragmatic necessity*. He's not saying differences don't matter. He's saying you can't eliminate them, so you'd better learn to route around them." I stopped walking. "Dangerous to everyone currently in power." I started walking again. "And potentially foundational to whatever comes next."


### **Edinburgh, 1776 CE**

Spinoza's ideas went underground. His books remained banned, but manuscripts circulated through networks of correspondence—Amsterdam to London, London to Paris, Paris to Edinburgh. A century after his death, I found his fingerprints in unexpected places.

In Edinburgh, a city that had reinvented itself as a center of learning after the 1707 union with England, I watched two men who had taken Spinoza's practical insight and developed it into something new.

David Hume was dying that August, and I managed to visit him twice before the end. What struck me was not his composure—I had seen enough death to know that serenity was possible—but his intellectual honesty. He had spent his career demolishing the foundations that other philosophers took for granted, and he remained cheerfully unbothered by the rubble.

"We cannot rationally justify induction," he had written. We assume the future will resemble the past, but we cannot *prove* it. We assume that causes produce effects, but all we actually observe is one thing following another. The foundations of human knowledge, examined closely, turned out to be custom and habit—not reason.

And yet the customs worked. That was Hume's great insight. We didn't need rational foundations. We needed practices that functioned, expectations that proved reliable, habits that coordinated behavior. The gap between what we could prove and what we could do was not a problem to be solved. It was the normal condition of a functioning society.

His friend Adam Smith had published *The Wealth of Nations* earlier that year. I read it in an Edinburgh tavern, watching the two of them through the window as Smith visited the dying Hume for what would be one of the last times. They had been correspondents for twenty-five years, debating economics and morals and the mechanics of human sympathy. Now Smith sat by his friend's bedside, and I sat with Smith's book, and between us we bracketed something important.

"It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that we expect our dinner," Smith had written, "but from their regard to their own interest."

The invisible hand. Self-interest producing public benefit. A system that coordinated millions of actors who didn't share beliefs, goals, or even knowledge of each other—through the mechanism of prices that no one designed and no one controlled.

"The result of human action but not human design," I said to *Just Checking*, closing the book. "That's the formula. That's what Spinoza was reaching for. You don't need shared beliefs. You don't need rational agreement on foundations. You need *mechanisms*—markets, customs, institutions—that produce coordination as a byproduct of people pursuing their own ends."

"Convenient for a species that can't agree on anything."

"Convenient for *any* species that can't agree on anything." I watched Smith emerge from Hume's house, his face drawn. "They're building the theory for a machine that doesn't require consensus. They just don't know that's what they're doing."

## **DISPATCH IV: Phase Transition (2024-2026 CE)**

The president's team refused. They argued over the seating arrangement—who would sit in the middle, in the "presidential chair." Annan called it "childish." The opposition walked out. The violence continued.

What struck me was what happened next. Annan played a song—something that had been composed for him when he left the United Nations. They signed a power-sharing agreement on February 28th. The incumbent remained president; the opposition leader became prime minister. Not justice, exactly. Not the clean victory that either side wanted. But coordination across a difference that had been killing people.

"It did not feel triumphant," Annan said afterward. "It had taken far too long."

That was the Diversity Machine working. Ugly, slow, full of compromise—but *working*. The trend line seemed positive. The machine was winning, or at least not losing.

Democratic backsliding across multiple continents. Authoritarian governance gaining ground. Nationalist movements explicitly rejecting the Diversity Machine's premises. The information environment—which I had documented in 1687 as the beginning of something promising—fractured into incommensurable shards. People living in the same cities, using the same technologies, inhabiting different realities.

The Legibility Machine cannot absorb the load. It was designed for bounded differences, not exponential divergence.

The Divergence Machine is what they need. Coordination without consensus. The Culture's architecture. They're building it blind, without knowing what they're building or where they're going.

The reactionary movements are attempts to restore the Legibility Machine—to make the world enumerable again. This cannot work; the complexity is structural. But the attempts can delay the transition, cause unnecessary suffering, possibly trigger catastrophic failure modes.

The question is navigation. The destination exists—I live there. The passage is what's uncertain.

They have surprised us before. The abolition of slavery was not inevitable; it happened because people chose it. The articulation of universal rights was not inevitable; it emerged because people fought for it. The survival of those principles through two world wars was not inevitable; it required continuous effort.

They keep choosing. That's what I've learned, watching them. The machine doesn't run itself. They run it. And enough of them, often enough, choose to run it toward something better.

I don't know if that will be sufficient. I know it has been sufficient so far.

Recommendation: Continued observation. Revised Contact protocols on standby—not because intervention is warranted, but because the transition, if successful, may complete faster than previous models predicted.

*—Sifan*
